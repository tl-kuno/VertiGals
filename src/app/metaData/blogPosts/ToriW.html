Meet Tori Ward, a Duluthian and nurse, who learned what the outdoors was after coming to college in Duluth and hasn't looked back since! Tori likes mountain biking, alpine skiing, playing guitar, camping, and of course being a new dog mom to Koda!

Read more about our #WCW below and see her unique advice to traveling climbers....

Where are you from?

White Bear Lake, MN. I moved here for undergrad at the College of St. Scholastica for the nursing program and to play in the jazz band, though I quickly found out that the outdoors scene would keep me here.

Have you had a chance to climb outside?

Yes - Palisade Head is my favorite local crag. Favorite route is still TBD.

How did you start rock climbing?  

I started climbing in college. The first week of school my freshman year I went to the CSS's climbing wall and had a job there a month later- I would later become an outdoor guide. Fun Fact- the first time I ever slept in a tent was my freshman year on a climbing trip in Devil’s Lake, Wisconsin.

What made you join Vertigals?

My friend Steph Hall asked me to come climb at VE on one of the Vertigals climb nights.

What is a climbing skill you are working on?

CRACK CLIMBING. I don't enjoy crack climbing right now but it's a crucial skill for Trad. I am trying to get more practice crack climbing as I climb outdoors.

Do you have any big climbing goals?

This summer I want to become more proficient at Trad, become a stronger leader, and just get outside climbing as much as I can.

Tell us about a lesson you learned once the "hard way"...

Don't trust “Canadians”. I went to Red Rocks with my climbing partner Sam Erickson. We were having a solid trip - we climbed a bunch and had a lot of fun. On our last day, we were just doing some sport climbs when I got a text on my phone. My credit card had been flagged for suspicious activity. I knew my wallet was in the rental car and Sam and I both thought someone had pulled a smash-and-grab on the rental car which we of course did not buy the extra insurance on. We were flying home on the red eye flight that night, so we wrapped up climbing for the day and hustled back to the car to find out what the damage was. When we got back to the car everything was totally fine. No smashed windows, no doors ajar, nothing. We couldn't figure out what had happened. I ripped open the glove compartment to find my wallet and my purse right where I had left them. I opened my wallet and found all my credit cards missing. I called and cancelled all my cards and ordered new ones and puzzled over how this happened. Then it dawned on me. When we were getting ready for climbing that morning we were approached by an older couple asking questions about our gear and what we were climbing. They asked us where we were from and we replied that we were from Minnesota. They excitedly replied they were from Canada and we were lulled into a false sense of security. We continued to talk and at one point were joined by another couple who were with the original people we were talking to, but had much less to say. They left shortly after and we headed out to the climbs; not thinking anything of our encounter at the time. Looking back we are pretty sure that we got straight up hustled. They did a great job keeping our attention on the distant cliffs and not to the open doors of our car directly behind us. We should have known better; they didn’t even have an accent. So, close your car doors, climb with your wallets, and don't let the “Canadians” fool ya!

What about an impactful or important climb?

My first multi pitch climb, Big Bad Wolf in Red Rocks. BBW is a 4-pitch sport route that goes at 5.9. This was my first time climbing out west, first multi pitch, so I was a little nervous. I was climbing well, nearing the top of the first pitch when I got to a move that not only definitely required a dynamic move, but I was about 12-15 feet above my last bolt. I tried to figure out a way to do the move static, but couldn’t and couldn’t bring myself to go for it. I ended up downclimbing to the bolt and getting lowered. That experience pushed me to get stronger at leading and get a better leader head. Between this and having my last day of climbing cut short in Red Rock I have some unfinished business that will definitely bring me back to Red Rock!

Have you had any climbing injuries?

I have been climbing for 6 years and I haven't been injured until this year. I was ice climbing at the Duluth Ice and Mix festival and it was the last climb of the day. As I was getting lowered I had a shelf of ice come down on top of me. I had a good sized goose egg on my head and a few cuts on my face. As the night progressed I started to have some concussion symptoms, so I had to call it an early night and get some good rest the week after. Overall I was really lucky and I was out climbing the next day at Shovel Point!

Because of the pandemic, I am...

Well so, I actually had COVID towards the start of the pandemic. (I don't recommend getting infected if you can help it.) So initially I was spending the pandemic having to quarantine. Since getting better I’ve been mountain biking and climbing. I also got a puppy in late May! Her name is Koda and she's a rescue pup - no clue about her breed. We've been together a few weeks and she's gone hiking, canoeing, camping, and is starting to spend more time around cliffs!

When travel is unrestricted again, I hope to go to:

I had a lot of trips cancelled due to Covid. This year I was planning on travelling once a month so Covid ruined my streak. So now I need to try and fit them back in this summer. I need to get back out to Red Rocks; Devil’s Tower; would love to fly down to Mexico and fit in an El Potrero Chico trip; and meet up with some friends in Utah for a mountain biking trip. I’ll have to add another ski trip this next year too since my trip to Salt Lake and Jackson Hole was cancelled.
