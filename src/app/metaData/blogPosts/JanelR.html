Starting the New Year off RIGHT with Janel Rieger. Janel is an absolute crusher calling the Twin Cities home. This gal knows how to WORK. She has some serious climbing sends under her belt and is hungry to learn more, conquer bigger walls, and make sure others are getting to come along for the ride! We are so excited for you to read all about our January 2021 WCW, Janel. Her interview was sprinkled with so many jewels of wisdom - sometimes I didn't even have a good question! On Read? Read on! (That was a belay joke...) Cheers!!

Fast Facts
Job Title: STEM education
Origin: New Ulm, MN
Favorite hobbies: Climbing, hams radios, topography, baking, and plants.
Favorite MN Route: Iron Maiden at Palisade
Favorite crag elsewhere: Anywhere in Squamish, B.C.
Climbing Origin: Minnesota, 2015.
If you had a million dollars, the first big purchase you would make is: Sprinter van and take a year off from life to just climb and connect with humans.

Why do you climb?
Climbing has allowed me time to spend with myself, my community, and outdoor spaces. Growing up as a 1st generation Mexican woman, the outdoors sports like climbing wasn’t really an option for me. Once I found my place in climbing I realized how much I loved being outside. Climbing allows me to explore these spaces, discover new friends, and connect with myself and other people from around the world.


Tell me about your first time climbing? Where? With who? Did you love it immediately?

First time I climbed was at Redwing! I was someone I was dating at the time but was on the outs with. I remember being really excited about it and as soon as I touched the wall, I was like okay, climbing… where have you been all my life? Lets hang out, a lot.

What's your favorite style of climbing? Any favorite technique or moves?

I LOVE CRACK CLIMBING! Especially offwidth climbing! Crack climbing is what tied the knot for me in climbing. Once I found it I became obsessed with it. I would make up drills at tcb on the crack wall, circuits at Taylors, and I de-stress by making crack gloves at home. There is something incredibly beautiful about how each and every crack is different for everyone depending on their body. For me it's an ongoing love. I feel the most stoked when I’m wedged halfway in a crack trying to figure out how to move like a quarter inch up in an offwidth. There’s not a crack I don’t want to climb. Finger, offhands, offwidth, invert, overhang- I love them all.

Any other weird hobbies when you aren't searching for cracks??

I have my class 2 or general class license for ham radio. It allows me to use all amateur radio bands and operating modes so I connect with people around the world. I sometimes go outside the city and see who is on their Hams radio. I’m also currently studying for the third level and its so nerdy but I love it.

Let's talk about SKILLS baby. What are you working on??

Mixed climbing for larger alpine objectives. This is my first season ice climbing and it has been phenomenal. At first, I was honestly very intimidated by ice climbing. It seemed saturated with tall white men and I wasn’t sure a small mexican woman could belong in this sport. However, I knew if I wanted to obtain my larger climbing objectives that this was the next step. Somehow it all clicked. I ended up connecting with Scott Backes, a legendary alpinist. He was offering to give away his nomics and we connected about the intimidation of ice and alpine climbing for BIPOC women. Along with being equipped with nomics, he offered to help teach me skills that would aid me to make the transition from trad to alpine objectives. He also offered to do a session with a few of my bipoc female friends interested in ice climbing. He introduced me to some incredible local ice climbers that have brought me into their circle and made me feel welcome. That whole experience has been life changing. I’ve been pulling all my energy into learning about alpine climbing, training aspects, rescues skills, and making my climbing dreams come true. You can now find me ice climbing every chance I have and don’t worry I’ve even somehow managed to find some bomber hand jams in that icy terrain.

Any specific places you have your eye on for these alpine skills??

Patagonia, Canadian rockies, and Himalayas. Mainly, I’d like to make the transition from trad climbing to larger alpine objectives. Also, conducting a topography study to establish routes in a climbing area called Cañon el Tajo in Mexico. This area is a little off the grid to outside climbers but the community there is beautiful. The rock type is granite and filled with cracks and slabs to be explored. It would be a life goal to have an FA in the country that my mother grew up in.

Grabbing an FA in Mexico as a 1st generation Mexican-American woman climber, that'd be seriously cool. It's no secret that climbing, and the outdoor community at-large, has gains to make still on inclusivity. Any first-hand experience with racism or sexism the crags?

Oh yes. I remember getting into climbing, it was incredibly exciting and then really frustrating. I wanted to start learning more about becoming an independent climber, meaning I could set up anchors, lead routes, and safely bring others along. Initially, I was met with a ton of male ego, talked down to, purposely excluded, and straight up feeling like I didn’t belong. It was disheartening, but eventually I met a few amazing fellow BIPOC climbers that became my climbing mentors and friends. Even as I progressed, I had partners gaslight me and talk down to me as a climber, saying things like “are you sure they said that because you’re a woman” and so on.
Recently, I was ice climbing and I had several white climbers talk at me when I brought up white privilege in the US and climbing. Rather than try to listen to some of the experiences I was trying to share as a brown girl and 1st gen American, I was interrupted, talked down to, and essentially mansplained what white privilege means. It was frustrating being I was the only POC at the moment. I felt so small and unwelcomed. At the end of it, we were able to come to a mutual understanding but this happens far too often to BIPOC people and it makes it hard to feel completely welcome. This, however, has fueled me to do the work I do within our climbing community and it’s made me even more passionate about climbing.

Oh yes, and do the work you have!! Editor confession, I heard of you BECAUSE of the great work you are doing with inclusiveness!! Please describe!

I had the honor to mentor some incredible women in climbing. My goals were to teach them to become safe and knowledgeable independent climbers. This work is so important to me. As I’ve mentioned prior, I was met with a lot of obstacles getting into climbing, especially trad climbing. I had some amazing mentors and they have motivated me to give back to my community. It is a privilege to be a climber and that alone should push us to share this with others. Yes it takes time, yes it takes patience, and yes sometimes you just want to stay in your bubble but it is 100 percent worth it. I love seeing these women out in the climbing world, sending it, teaching others, and showing the climbing community we belong in this space. 

Every time one of the women I’ve mentored sends me pictures of anchors they’ve built, climbs they’ve sent, or just plain stoke about something they have done in climbing my climber heart feels completely full and accomplished

___________

So, where are we at with diversity in the outdoors and where can we go?

We are getting there, but there is a lot of work to do. Here’s the thing, until we actually start to listen to the underrepresented community and let them become leaders in all aspects of it we are not there yet. A few things to highlight, geotagging became a big topic this year with a lot of people wanting to start getting outdoors more with COVID-19. There was a pushback from the outdoor community to stop geotagging and keep these places hidden. This kind of idea perpetuates that only certain people should be allowed in outdoor spaces. Those certain people tend to not include people of color. Often, being knowledgeable of how to navigate the outdoors was not a privilege we grew up with. At the end of the day, it's gatekeeping and elitism of public lands. Everyone, no matter their upbringing, should be allowed the chance to find and explore these areas. Instead of pushing an anti-geotagging movement, we, as an outdoor community should be pushing education on outdoor ethics and how to leave no trace.

Great example of the "#nogeotag" working AGAINST allowing new voice to fall in love with our public spaces and the outdoors. Where are we at in climbing, more specifically?

Climbing has a long history of being predominantly white and male. That is beginning to shift. However, the origins of climbing have shaped a lot of how climbers act and treat others in our community. There is a lot of exclusiveness, negativity towards being a new, lack of mentorship, and giving back to our community.

I will begin with saying I have met and connected with so many amazing humans in the last 5 years. They have shown me how beautiful our community can be. That being said, there's still a lot we have to work on. For example, when I started looking for what gyms I wanted to be a part of, I started climbing at one of the smaller gyms locally. I was met with a lot of exclusive climbers, ego driven beta sprayers, and white girl clicks that didn't seem to have room for me to climb with them. I thought, maybe it was just me. I have a pretty loud personality and often am told I should quiet down or calm down by these other climbers. Then I talked with other BIPOC female climbers and they had similar experiences in these same climbing spots. They felt like outsiders and ended up finding themselves climbing alone. This made my heart hurt.

It only takes a few moments to make someone feel welcome, it can be a "hey can I work on this boulder with you" or "I haven't met you yet welcome to the community." We have to change how we view and treat others, this takes time but it’s worth investing our time into.

_____________

Any wisdom for our readers on how to improve inclusivity and therefore diversity in climbing??

As a climbing community we have so many opportunities to do this and it begins with us, as individuals. A big portion of this is being open to climbing with people outside of your group. Being willing to bring a new person in, teach them some of the ropes, and introduce them to others they might connect with. Climbing is an intimidating sport. It's dangerous, it takes you to far away places, and it pushes you physically and emotionally. That's why mentorship is so vital to keep our spaces clean, safe, and for the future of developing this sport.

Most of the women I’ve mentored didn’t think they would ever be leading trad or going on a climbing trip. Once they started, they became integral parts of our community, from buying state park passes, picking up trash, to becoming more aware and vocal of why we need to be conscious of climate change and preserving these public lands. This is incredibly important not only to climbing but for the future of these lands. Lastly, we need more access to education, we need to be highlighting what BIPOC climbers are doing, and continue to support climbing organizations dedicated to bringing more diversity to climbing. 

SPEAKING OF HIGHLIGHTING AWESOME BIPOC CLIMBERS, you've been on some BIG WALLS! Where all at??

This Fall I started to focus on transition to big wall projects for Yosemite. I spent a large portion of my climbing time in North Conway, NH. I did the Prow, mordor wall, and then climbed in an area called cannon cliffs. One of my favorite routes was Moby Grape and well I absolutely adore all East coast climbing.

Okay, what about a "Small Wall" like....maybe...you know, how you crushed El Matador on Devil's Tower!

IT WAS SO FREAKING INCREDIBLE! I got there early, it was 50 degrees, so send temps. I had a triple rack of 00 to .75, doubles up to 2, and two sets of nuts. Then looked up at it and was in awe. There was something so beautiful about this climb and even this moment in my climbing for me. I definitely have those moments before a climb of am I ready for this, is this too much, and other nervous inner banter. This time, as soon as I looked at the line, I just knew I wanted more than anything to be climbing that. Like this climb was made for me. Plus, I had an incredibly supportive climbing partner with me that day and that makes a world of a difference.

The money pitch started out with tiny fingers locks, a few hand jams, and a lot of delicate corner toe placements. It wasn’t until about a quarter to halfway up that I was able to fully commit to the stem and that was just fine. Once the stemming began it was all about fighting that endurance pump, trusting my finger locks, and smears. I truly loved it and despite the calf pump and some sacrificial blood from the finger locks I never stopped smiling. The happy send vibes lasted for months and it is everything it's hyped up to be.

What's next on the horizon? More projects?

I’m applying to take the rock guide course and plan on spending the next year working on objectives to help train me for big expedition climbs. That means alpine climbing, more ice climbing, more mentoring women of color in climbing, and more showing the climbing community BIPOC climbers belong outside. To conclude, I’m so thankful to be a part of this community and feel honored to be able to spend my time with all of you on the mountains.
