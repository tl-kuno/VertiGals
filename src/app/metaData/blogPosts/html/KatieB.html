Meet our September WCW Katie! Katie is a college English professor and competition dance coach who likes to climb, dance, scuba dive, ski, hike, read, knit, and write guide books in her spare time! She recently moved back to the Twin Cities after being in Duluth for over 15 years! Read on to learn about her guide book writing, scary adventure stories, and love for all things CRACK Climbing!

Fast Facts:
Origin: Twin Cities
Hobbies: Climbing, Dancing, Cold Water Scuba Diving, Cross Country Skiing, Hiking, Reading, and Knitting
Fav Duluth business: Duluth Cider and Zenith Bookstore
1st Purchase you'd make if you had a million dollars: Finish my PhD, then teach all online just part time so I can travel and adventure more!

What made you want to join Vertigals?
Meeting all of the amazing ladies!

You live in the Twin Cities now, but lived in Duluth for over 15 years! What brought you to Duluth and what brought you back home to the Cities?

I moved to Duluth to go to UMD and stayed for 15 years. I recently got married and had to move back to the cities, but I'm in Duluth at least twice a month and have a key and a room, so I'm basically a Duluthian still. I started climbing at UMD - the North Shore Climbers club night, 2004 sometime.

What's your favorite style of climbing? Any favorite technique or moves?

Crack climbing. Ooooh yes. A beautiful hand jam or fingers that goes for days. I also like the fight of offwidth climbing and chimneys. Overall I love multi-pitch trad, preferably miles from the road so I have to hike in, really get to know my partner, get an alpine start, probably be cold en route, eat tuna at pitch 5, lose my way, climb up chimneys covered in bird poop (sorry about that one, Angie), say hi to a bat three pitches up inside of a crack while on lead, and hopefully top out to incredible views and probably wind.
And, of course, probably getting lost on the way down. Or rained on. Or hailed on.

Basically any sort of Type-2 fun and I'm in.

Ever had any scary experiences while Multi-Pitching or being int he Mountains??

I was in Yosemite on a rest "weekend" (we'd do 5 days "on" of climbing and 2 days "off"), which meant that my partner and I were hiking peaks that were 4th class and below. We started up one of the peaks near Lee Vining in beautiful weather with hiking poles and overnight backpacking gear. Getting in to the peak was fantastic- beautiful hike, amazing mountain views, and camping on snow, which I loved. We hit the peak the next morning and started down, which was where the trouble was. Because it was so sunny and beautiful the day before, the big steep snow field we had post-holed up the day before had melted and then frozen overnight into a giant sheet of ice probably 2 football fields high, with steep chossy cliffs on either side at a 45 degree angle with no real way down. We didn't have crampons, so walking straight down the steep ice field was out of the question. We finally decided to very carefully hike along the edge of the cliff and the ice, which worked until we were about halfway down and the ice had separated away from the wall because of the warmth of the day heating up the rock the ice was pushed up against. At that point, we had to climb down between the ice and the wall, shoving our packs up against a vertical piece of ice with our hands and feet pushing up against the wall. We stayed as close to the top of the ice as possible while basically chimneying against the ice. If we had fallen, we would have slipped down between the ice and wall for a good 15-20 feet before sliding under the ice, which would not have been great. Needless to say, probably one of the most terrifying moments I've ever had. We had to shuffle along like that for probably 20 feet until we could crawl out again. I had a new appreciation for my pizza that night to celebrate not sliding under the ice.

Favorite Trad ever?
Regular Route on Fairview Dome in Tuolumne Meadows, Yosemite NP. Beautiful moderate Multi-pitch adventure climbing is my favorite :)

Tell me about your "Cold Water Scuba Diving" because that sounds like a PEAK Duluth hobby:

In Spring of 2019 (we didn't get to do it this year because of COVID), I went scuba diving in Lake Superior under the ice when it was completely frozen. I was with an amazing group of dive friends, and despite the outside -10 degree air (and -20 or something windchill), we cut a hole in the ice and did dry-suit diving under the ice. Diving for me is kind of terrifying and forces me to mentally keep my brain calm, so diving into this cold silent world under the ice was exhilarating and daunting (and really cold), and I'm very proud of myself to mentally do that. Plus blowing bubbles under the ice and playing with them where they collect is really fun.

Sometimes women climbers face some overt or covert sexism...what has been your experience with that?
I was a small blond female working in a climbing gym environment, and when I was on trips around the US climbing in the Wind River Range or the Tetons or Yosemite, people usually assumed that my male partner was the one leading or in charge.

What did you take away from that?
I was definitely more timid at first, but the more it happened (which was a lot) the more I grew accustomed to correcting or questioning someone's word choice. I'm an English teacher, so word choice really matters to me. The changing conversations have been so refreshing, but there's definitely room for improvement.

Word on the street is that you are redoing the Minnesota Climbing Guide Book! That's awesome! Tell us about your decision to take that on?

It was a combination of events. Falcon Guidebooks put out the call for an author when the previous author decided he didn't want to do the new update. I originally did not want to take it on; I know how much work it is to do a nonfiction book like this. I wrote a guidebook a couple years ago called North Shore Adventures that gives suggestions for the best climbing, hiking, mountain biking, canoe/kayaking, and scuba diving from Duluth to the Canadian Border on the North Shore. It's a lot of time, research, interviews, organization (LOTS of organization), and work. I knew this would be the same. Luckily, I had encouragement from others that I would (hopefully!) be a good fit. First was the encouragement from the photographer for the new update, Angie Jacobsen. She would partner with me to take on the parts of the book that I knew would be overwhelming for me with a full teaching load--the photos and the topos--and I could focus on the writing, revisions, and the research. I had a lot of climber friends encourage me as well since I spent years climbing on the North Shore and I am very talkative with climbers I don't know, so Angie and I interviewed with Falcon, I submitted a writing sample, and we were hired.

Have there been some challenges to redo the guide book?
The anticipation to properly make a manuscript that I hope really represents Minnesotan climbers has been a little daunting. I want the guidebook to be useful, thorough, informative, and let the character of each place be truly captured through the descriptions of the area. I want all Minnesota climbers to feel proud of their local crag when they read the finished book. So no pressure… right? :) Another is the sheer amount of organization of LOTS of details involved can at times be overwhelming. Last, getting out to climb during COVID has made things far more challenging.

What is the best part about redoing the guide book?
I absolutely LOVE getting to interview climbers and first ascensionists about their passion projects, their favorite movements on the climbs in our state, and the sport we all love so much. I also love that I get to go climb a lot. I like crack climbing and offwidth and adventure climbing, so climbing all of these rarely-climbed and dirty routes is really fun for me! :)

Soooooo do you have favorite route or problem in MN?
Definitely, but it's like asking to name a favorite child...

Nonetheless, do you have one??
Poseidon's Adventure: 5.11 finger crack is my jam (though really any Palisade route that eats gear).

Why did you choose to redo the guide book as opposed to only updating an online platform like Mountain Project?

I'm probably not a good person to ask about this. I collect guidebooks from every place I've climbed in the US and in the world (If anyone needs to borrow a Kalymnos, Greece or Dolomites guidebook, just ask!), and I'm an English teacher that loves old fashion in-my-hand books. I make notes in my books and press leaves between the pages and put a sticker from Miguel's in the inside cover, and when I borrow out my guidebooks, I encourage friends to leave traces of their own adventures in the pages with pen, pencil, food and coffee stains, or mementos. I've also climbed in a lot of places with no signal to get Mtn Project. I believe a guidebook should be used in partnership with an online platform like Mountain Project. I absolutely love Mountain Project as a collective forum for updates, beta, suggestions for campgrounds, etc, but nothing beats shoving a guidebook on the top of your pack for an old school reference tool.

When travel is unrestricted again, where do you hope to travel?
Squamish! I haven't been able to get a summer month-long trip there yet to enjoy aaaaall the crack climbing. Or the Bugs. Basically Canada. I've spent over a year of my life traveling in Canada already, and I need to go back.

So when you aren't being a professor, being an author, or being a coach....any skills you are working on in your spare time in climbing?
I'm not great at dynamic moves or the parkour-type bouldering stuff. I'm short with a negative ape index, and with my dance background I'm way better at static controlled moves or moves that require flexibility. I live near MBP and have been able to practice some more dynamic movements, but I'm still terrible at them. I'm also hyperflexible, so if I come down from the top of a boulder bad or miss a parkour move, things can pop out of the socket easily. Jumping for a big parkour move is a possible months-long recovery. Dynamic moves are a mental training thing just as much as physical for me.
