It’s starting to feel a lot like summer! Which means outdoor climbing, paddling, and camping! Thinking about camping got me to thinking about campfires. Thinking about campfires made me think of our responsibility to Leave No Trace and prevent forest fires! And that got me thinking of our WCW Kate! Kate is a wildland FIREFIGHTER. Dope, right?! Right now she's out in Washington State and we were able to catch up with her right before she went offline for a week! So, Kate, you'll see this in a few days :) Read more to learn about our #WCW Kate below!

Fast facts:

Job Title: Wildland Firefighter - Fire Ecology Team
Origin: Detroit, MI
Current Home: I've been moving around a lot for a while. I call Duluth, MN home base, but I currently live in Marblemount, WA
Favorite hobbies: Everything and anything outdoors and baking sourdough bagels and bread!


You've had Duluth as your home base for a few years, but you travel and live lots of places! Any favorites? 
YES! I feel so lucky and privileged to live a life where I travel all the time for my recreation, but also for my job. I don't really feel like I have a favorite place...but my top 5 (in no order): Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, AK, Wind River Range, WY, The Ruby Mountains, NV, Copper Harbor/Keweenaw Peninsula, MI, and the East Sierras (Mono Lake, Bishop, Yosemite!)

How do you stay in shape or train for your athletic hobbies?

I have the privilege of being able to use my job as a way to train and stay "in shape." Being a wildland firefighter allows me to work my body pretty hard on a daily basis. So, luckily, it's easy to stay motivated when your job, personal safety and crew safety relies on it. 

Any big adventures on the horizon? 

I have been dreaming/planning of bikepacking the Baja Divide in Mexico! A friend and I started planning about a year ago to complete it last winter (and we were lined up to help raise funds for the Cairn Project, which is an awesome account to follow!) but with COVID we decided it wasn't the best timing. Still hoping to do it in the near future, but we'll see. 

Would you call yourself a climber? 

Whenever I am asked this question, I immediately answer "no, not really, but kinda." I'm at a point in climbing where I enjoy it, and I'm starting to lead super easy routes. Which an exciting point to be at, but because I move so much for work, I feel like I have never really had a constant group of people to mentor/teach me.

What are some skills you don’t have right now, but are working on? How are you working on that? 

I sort of feel like an imposter being on the VertiGals page because I don't climb that much...but I am trying to start lead climbing some easy routes and trying to find my "flow" climbing. It can be really hard to work on a partner sport when you move to a new place every few months and have to establish a climbing partner/mentor and somehow weasel your way into these already established relationships. 


Oh my goodness, I bet SO. MANY. of our readers can relate to this feeling! For the record - you don't have to send a 5.TEEN in order to be a climber (lord knows I don't send those grades!) and certainly not to be our WCW. Instead of sending it on rock, you 'send' it on....ummm wildfires?!

You are basically a professional fire.....studier? fighter?

Yeah! I usually tell people I'm a nerdy wildland firefighter, but, to put it simply, I am on a fire ecology crew. I have this awesome position where I go out and fight wildfire about half the time, but I also get to collected pre/post vegetation data to see how our forests are changing after a fire goes through. Which is such a cool position, and in my opinion, super important for our public land with climate change. (IT'S AWESOME!)

Your career has focused on the outdoors and the environment, have you personally noticed a big change?  

Yes. This last year with COVID, has had an intense impact on public lands.

Any recommendations for podcasts/blogs for other VertiGals? 

If people are looking for really well done public land/ecology/just interesting sciencey/societal podcasts I would recommend Threshold, Life with Fire, or (for VERY nerdy science peeps check out The Field Guides podcast). 

What's a piece of advice you'd give people that want to adventure and live a little less 9-to-5?  

I think I would tell people it seems glamorous, (especially the whole van life thing) but it can be really hard and it's not really glamorous at all. It's a lot of sitting in laundromats, trying to find potable water, and finding "the best dispersed camping spot." I think the main takeaway is to try to not have any pre-established ideas of what "adventure" looks like because adventure is whatever you want it to be.
