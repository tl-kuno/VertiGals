Meet our November WCW Liz! Liz hails from 'Bama originally but calls the Twin Cities home now. She works as a microbiologist by day, and a climbing CRUSHER by night and weekend. Liz [and October WCW Rachelle] got the send of Oz – (AKA The Road To Emerald City) at Palisade Head this fall, and we knew we needed to interview her immediately. When not climbing, she likes to bike, read, travel, and garden. Read on to learn about her experience with subtle sexism, her send of a Palisade classic, and why you should absolutely go get on a project like Oz!

Warm Ups
Favorite MN problem: I’d have to say The Woodchipper at Sawmill. It’s tall, has a good landing, and just enough possibility of a heartbreaker finish.
Favorite crag elsewhere: Fontainebleau, for the pastries as much as the climbing.
First purchase you would make with $1,000,000: Selfishly, tuition for grad school… I miss academia, but I definitely don’t miss student loans.

How'd you get into climbing?
I actually started climbing in Hong Kong in 2005. I was studying abroad and kind of randomly signed up to join the climbing club there as a way to make some friends who weren’t just other exchange students.

What's your favorite style of climbing?
I love steep, tricky bouldering. Roofs, slopers, compression, and toe hooks bring me joy. A general aversion to sharp holds means I can’t stand those vertical crimpfests that lots of folks think I should love because I’m a woman. You know what I love? Keeping the skin on my fingertips.

Have you ever experienced sexism in the outdoors community?
Ah, I think we all have at some point. The bigger, obvious displays of sexism haven’t bothered me as much because those are ridiculous enough to either laugh at or immediately call out. It’s the frequent, little, insidious things people say or do that add up to become the most damaging because they’re this constant voice of doubt. It’s when people assume less about you and your abilities as a default. It’s the times on climbing trips when I’m projecting a hard boulder while Pi, my husband, is hanging out not climbing, and guys come up and ask Pi questions about the climb every time because it’s automatically assumed that I wouldn’t be doing something difficult. It’s the fact that I still get asked if I need rental shoes at the climbing gym, while he’s never been asked. There’s nothing wrong with being a beginner, but as a climbing community, we HAVE GOT to get away from that assumption that women=unskilled.

PREACH. IT. I'd say you did your part to showcase how wrong that assumption is this year when you got together with other women and went for Oz at Palisade Head. Let's discuss!

Why did that route become a project for you?

There were a few things that contributed to it. It was mostly because Rachelle was psyched and asked me to try it with her. She also happened to ask at a time when I was feeling frustrated with bouldering and minor injuries, so trying a project that’s basically the opposite of that (a long endurance trad climb) was appealing. We also learned that apparently no women had led Oz before, which seemed crazy to us because there are lots of women in the MN climbing community who are more than capable of doing that. I think as women, we’re socialized from a really young age to avoid trying things we don’t think can do, or that we’re not 100% qualified for. But I mean, if you can try it in a way that’s reasonably safe, why not give it a go? Aren’t you curious? I hope that some day, what we did will be utterly unremarkable, because so many other women will be crushing much harder climbs here.

Girl, I feel that in my soul....
Right now, though, it is quite remarkable...in fact, please make more remarks about your send. Specifically, how'd it all come together?

I spent so much time stressing out the week leading up to the send that I must have been out of adrenaline when it was time to climb. I was a little rattled in the beginning because I racked some gear out of order and had to fix it at the first good rest, but I was able to pull my head back together and the rest of the climb felt surprisingly relaxed. It was one of those great climbing experiences were you’re kind of just an observer along for the ride while your body just does what it already knows to do. In a funny way, being a person who is naturally anxious has given me some good mental coping skills for stressful climbing situations.

For all our readers who are inspired now to go for it, describe why you choose to go for Oz as opposed to some other leads at Palisade.

Oz is a beautiful climb. It was appealing to me because the rock quality is good throughout most of the route, it has cool movement in the lower section, and while there are some runouts, the gear is solid and the falls are clean. Neither Rachelle nor I had really gotten into trad before because we weren’t interested in doing climbs that didn’t feel physically challenging. For me, having no endurance, Oz felt challenging enough to be interesting, but safe because you’re so high up by the time you’re truly pumped and the falls are totally fine. It was perfect.

What was the most challenging part of working Oz?
Oh man. The fact that I was totally out of my element! I had to learn how to top rope solo, set up anchors that wouldn’t kill us, place gear that would probably stay, learn to actually rest in hand jams, and learn how to self-rescue by jugging the rope when I inevitably forgot to place directionals on the way down. It was overwhelming at times! It’s humbling to be in a sport for so long and in some ways still be a complete beginner.

And what was the best part of working that project?
I’m used to mostly working on boulders by myself, so the best part was definitely the social aspect. We had a great little crew of friends meeting up there most weekends who brought their psych, snacks, and a not-insignificant amount of booze and good-natured heckling.

Because of the pandemic, how are you spending your free time?
Because of the pandemic, the public health infectious disease lab where I work has become an all-COVID-19-all the-time stress hive. So, when I’m not working, I’m doing everything in my power to disengage from that. In addition to climbing, I’ve been reading a lot of escapist fiction books that usually involve time or inter-dimensional travel. The weirder, the better. They make real life feel comfortingly mundane.

What's next on the horizon? More projects?
For me, it’s back to bouldering for the fall through spring. I’d love to pick out another trad project for next summer, though. Maybe something with more powerful moves and not as long. I’m open to suggestions!
