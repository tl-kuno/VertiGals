It’s April already (HUH?!) and that means shoulder season is squarely upon us here in the North... As we transition from winter to summer, you might be thinking about all your gear needs. We are, too! So this month we are featuring local Gear Guru Brooke Wetmore! Brooke is the Co-Founder of Great Lakes Gear Exchange who, when she's not being a bada$$ entrepreneur and business owner, you can find canoeing, biking, birding, and skiing! Read more to learn about our #WCW Brooke and her rad business.

Fast Facts!

Origin:
Twin Cities originally, Duluth is fully my home now though :)

Rock climbing origin:
I climb off and on, I typically get really into climbing for a while and then fall out of the habit for a bit and then get back into it again :) It typically has to do with if I have a great climbing partner that I make a habit with. I started climbing while I was a student at UMD. I was utterly terrified of it at first. When I am in the climbing habit, I climb at VE.

First big purchase you would make if you had $1,000,000:
A kevlar canoe.

Ahh, you are a big water fan we see. Any big paddling accomplishments?!

A few summers ago I was invited to paddle 230 miles of the Colorado River through the Grand Canyon. I spent much of my time the summer before in my kayak, honing and getting my paddling abilities in shape to prepare myself for the big, high volume western river (a different experience than the low volume, rocky creeks of our area). The biggest rapid on the river is called Lava Falls. From the moment I agreed to go on the trip it started occupying a corner of my mind.

... As the trip approached I started watching youtube videos of others running the rapid. When we arrived in Flagstaff to prep for the trip prior to heading up to the put-in, conversation about Lava Falls began. About half of our crew had paddled the river before (and had done the rapid), the other half of us were newbies. We had about 2 weeks of paddling before we got to the rapid, hardly a day went by that Lava Falls wasn't discussed by the crew - what were the lines, what did the features look like, what had past experiences been, etc. We finally made it to the top of the rapid where I spent close to half an hour just looking at the few 100 feet of river stretched out before me. As my fellow paddlers began loading in their boats I figured I was as ready as I would ever be. I followed my best friend into the drop, it was over in a flash. I followed the exact line I intended to and (bonus) stayed up right the whole time!

So, all told - I spent a year and half thinking about this rapid, hours talking about it, half an hour looking at it, and 8 seconds actually paddling the damn thing.

So when you aren't planning adventures on white water, you are 1 of the 2 founders of Duluth's Great Lakes Gear Exchange - Describe its origin story!

Both Emily and I had experienced similar stores in other small, outdoor oriented towns and both of us thought while we were there, "Duluth totally needs something like this!" We started business planning many years ago. After planning everything we possibly could on paper, we tested it out with pop-up sales that we hosted at the Duluth Folk School. Pop-ups were promising so we took the plunge to open the storefront in November of 2019.

What was an early challenge or obstacle you faced after starting GLGE?
Spending all day talking to people about gear, but not having the time/energy to get out and do it myself when things were crazy busy as we were getting off the ground!

Oh, 100% that would be such a bummer! But you gotta have that hustle!

What would you say are some of the GLGE's causes? How do you support those causes?


Ending elitism in the outdoors. Environmental Education. Dismantling systems of oppression. Creating a healthy work culture. Being stewards of natural resources. Being a platform/microphone for important causes and by directly contributing $$s to causes we care about

Have you ever experienced sexism or related issues in the outdoors community? As a woman owner of an adventure store?

When Emily and I started discussing the idea of opening the Great Lakes Gear Exchange, one thing that drove us was a mission to make outdoor adventure more accessible. I can't remember a time of experiencing overt sexism until I was working in the outdoor industry. Sometimes that sexism was completely innocent by the perpetrator (whenever I was unloading a trailer full of kayaks in a public parking lot (which was literally my job) ALWAYS a guy would come over and try to help), but sometimes it was really overt (a boy scout troop leader literally asked the clerk at the gas station for their opinion about paddling conditions on the lake when I had made a call that conditions were too rough for us to go out).

I have a crew of dudes that I do most of my outdoor adventuring with. They are all incredibly kind, they encourage me to push myself, they are fantastic humans. The fact that most of my adventuring is incredibly male dominated is not something that I think too much about UNTIL I find myself surrounded by other women. How empowering!! I remember one time I was out kayaking with a big crew, and one of the other women suggested we break off and do a ladies paddle. I again didn't think too much of it, thought it was "cute." But when we were paddling and encouraging each other, it was a completely different experience. I felt empowered, I felt like I was speaking a different language with these women. I get warm fuzzies just thinking about it!

WE COULDN'T AGREE MORE. There's something magical that happens when a group of gals gets together to adventure.

What makes you proud to own/co-found the GLGE?

The most rewarding customer interactions I have are when a customer comes in and is clearly uncomfortable because they are interested in trying a new activity but feel like they don't know the first thing about the gear they will need. When they leave and express that they learned a lot and you can physically see the empowerment they are feeling to go out and try something new, I love it!
