Meet our October WCW Rachelle! Rachelle is a registered dietitian in the Twin Cities who we got the chance to climb with only days after her lead of Oz - (AKA The Road To Emerald City) at Palisade Head. When not crushing, she likes to belay for total strangers like me, eat vegetables, grow plants, be active, volunteer in her community, and travel! Read on to learn about her send of a Palisade classic, her excitement for learning about self-rescue, and dreams of travel post-pandemic.

Where are you from?: Minneapolis area
Favorite hobbies: eating vegetables, growing plants, being active, volunteering in my community
Favorite route or problem in MN: 2018 traverse challenge at the MN Climbing Coop
Favorite crag elsewhere: Spearfish, SD

When and where did you originally start climbing: In high school actually, when I worked at a fitness facility in the kid’s programming department

If you suddenly had an extra $1,000,000 how would you spend it?
First: payoff my student loans (responsible, I know).
Second: two one way tickets to travel with my partner

Travel! Man, we sure do miss travel! When travel is unrestricted again, where do you hope to go?
Back to Kenya, and surrounding countries. I studied abroad at the end of college and haven’t been able to get back since. Missing ugali, friends and matatu life. I’m eager to show my partner a place that was such an important place of growth for me.

So since we can't really travel these days, how are you spending your free time?
For me, the best part of traveling is slowing down enough to explore the people, culture, food, and day to day life of new places. Limited travel due to the pandemic has given my partner and I a reason to do this at home in Minneapolis, setting out on long days with a loose agenda and a backpack full of snacks. We spent multiple days walking for hours through neighborhoods and areas we often don’t spend time, in the same way we do when we travel. There is so much beauty in the world when I remind myself to stop and enjoy it. Also, Palisade Head became my home away from home on weekends when I wasn’t working at the hospital.

SPEAKING OF PALISADE HEAD - tell me about your decision to project Oz (AKA The Road to Emerald City) a 5.12b up the shore!
In 2019, I pledged to myself that I would say yes to every opportunity I was offered. Among many amazing experiences during this time, I got my first taste of climbing at Palisade Head. All of my yes’s in 2019 resulted in an unexpected amount of desire to learn more and expand my skill set - and in late fall, I told a handful of people about an impromptu goal of leading Oz on gear in 2020. A few were really psyched and some were really doubtful given my non-existent trad experience, which made me double down on my newly announced project. Liz naively agreed to sign onto the project with me, and as a team, we applied for a Live Your Dream grant through the American Alpine Club (which was not awarded to anyone due to COVID). As we talked about being a team, and the process, and bringing other women up with us, learning new skills and acknowledging where our personal weaknesses were, we found this project represented way more than just another climb on our resume.

Why did you choose to go for Oz as opposed to some other leads at Palisade?
The movement on this climb is incredible - a bit hard down low with sustained movement through a middle crack section that goes on for eternity. Also, a couple of bolts protect the bottom which minimizes decking potential and the line takes gear really well. I learned to trad climb while also performing near my physical max, and unintentionally tested out nearly all of the upper placements with some sizable whips (for the record: they all held, and I had A+ belayers).

What has been the hardest part about working this project?
The most daunting part of the process was rappelling 180 feet to Lake Superior’s shore and knowing there was absolutely no easy way out. About two months into the season, I was on route, less than halfway up and really struggling to put forth a decent effort. Out loud, I asked myself if I really wanted this, and if I didn’t, to go home for good. I replied to myself, also out loud, “I came to f***ing party” and this became TeamLadyOz’s mantra - showing up fully committed and ready to put the work in. We stopped doubting the possibility of finishing this project after that weekend and trusted ourselves, our team and our abilities. And ultimately, we did it our way: we learned to trad climb on a route closer to our upper limit, placed hexes on route and used them for anchors, took whips on gear, took sh*t from nobody, told rude tourist assholes to **** off when they were treating us like zoo animals.

Clearly you DID come to party, because you got the send!! Did you feel in the zone on your trad lead? Any snaffus or obstacles to overcome?
The weekend prior to sending, I had back-to-back heart breaker attempts. My send go was calm and overdue, and I was so relieved when it was done. Double rainbows, dark clouds and sprinkles appeared shortly after!

Other than that feeling of relief after all that hard work, what was the best part of working Oz?
The best part of the project was getting to climb with Liz Horn consistently for a season, witnessing her process and belaying for her send. She’s been pushing boundaries in MN climbing for a long time, and with a FFA of Oz, she’s proven that she can do it across styles.
The best part of the process was building “Teamladyoz” in which everyone involved had important contributions. Teamladyoz provided a live example of women supporting women, and why representation matters in the community - people will aspire for bigger goals when they see themselves represented. And we still have so much work to do to make sure everyone can feel this.

What are some skills you don’t have right now, but are working on? How are you working on that?
Safety, safety, safety. I lack sufficient self rescue and first aid skills and plan to take a wilderness first responder course within the next year to address my incompetency! And as part of this, I’m learning more about emotional trauma, grief and mental health issues associated with climbing and the experiences we may be a part of (or in my case, see friends be involved with). I owe it to my partners, friends and community to be prepared, should an accident happen.

What's next on the horizon? More projects?
Ask Liz, I’m letting her choose the next one! In the interim, paying back all of the love to the #Teamladyoz supporters!
