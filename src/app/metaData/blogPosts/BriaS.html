Bria Schurke was basically raised in the wild by her expedition parents and sled dogs. She then chose to keep living in the wild as a NOLS instructor. Now she's back in Duluth to climb, ski, mountain bike, and be an all around b--a-- as a P.A. (physician's assistant) in Emergency Medicine. We caught up with her as she was returning from a mountain bike session on the new Jackpot trail in Lutsen. Read to learn more about her thoughts on climbing, COVID, and community....

Fast facts
Job Title: Emergency Medicine Physician Assistant
Origin: Ely, MN
Hobbies: rock climbing, nordic skiing, mountain biking
Favorite wilderness area: The Wind River Range
Favorite Style of climbing: Trad alpine climbing
First time climbing: Carlton Peak in Tofte was my first outdoor climb when I was 14 years old.
Local business I love is: Dovetail Coffee Shop at the Duluth Folk School
If I had a million dollars, I would: Support a local climbing gym that focused on being an inclusive and community centric space

I spend my time climbing as opposed to other activities because:
I love the relationships I build with my climbing partners, the adventures and stories shared, the problem solving, the physical challenge, the art of the movement, and simply the time spent reflecting in beautiful landscapes.

My favorite “style” of climbing is:

Trad Alpine Climbing. In fact, the last opportunity I had to travel I went to the Wind River Mountain Range in Wyoming to do some alpine climbing. I went with my friend Karina. The Winds are a long, narrow span of granite cliffs that are really the closest place we have to this type of amazing alpine climbing. It was awesome going with another female, too! We went at our own pace, which honestly was a pretty intense pace! We had really nice weather the whole time, so we constantly found ourselves pushing deeper and deeper into the range! Each day we climbed in remote and beautiful areas, and just went for it every day! We hit a good flow. It reminded me that we have some really tough women in Minnesota. Minnesota may not have mountains, but I think we have a lot of women that are hungry for the real adventure climbing that mountain ranges offer.

A change in the Duluth/North Shore climbing community I would like to see is:
I would love to see a climbing gym (VE or otherwise) built in West Duluth, that was an inclusive and community centric space that both novices and experts alike to build community and build their climbing skills; a space where people of all ages, backgrounds, and abilities felt excited and welcome to be a part of. It'd also be great to see our Duluth community grow so that mentorship and menteeship can flourish. The lack of a mentor can be a big barrier for people to get into climbing and mountain adventure more broadly. We need to make sure people can safely get to that next level of adventure, if they want it!

When travel is unrestricted again, I hope to go to:
The Bugaboos in Canada. I have never been here but I have a close friend to Spokane, so we plan on going into the Bugaboos together at some point. The Bugaboos are known for being the "mecca" of adventure climbing in the Northwest. It's a stunning area. We don't have any specific routes planned - in fact I kind of love just getting into the wilderness with an open agenda. Just to feel strong, badass, and have all the Type 1 fun we can!

NOLS, or the National Outdoor Leadership School is a non-profit outdoor education program that teaches folks how to survive outside. You were a NOLS instructor. Tell me how you got into that:

My dad is a fairly well-known adventurer and expedition leader. So, I grew up in a unique family that had adventure and extreme wilderness as a mainstay - I went to the North Pole when I was seven years old and I had been in Siberia eight times before I graduated from high school. I knew of NOLS growing up. After college I wasn't sure what I wanted to do with my life, but I knew I wanted to spend my days outside, so I took an instructor course. I am really lucky to have had the opportunity to work at NOLS. It's not a well-paying job, but I could choose to be a wanderer, living out of my car with a low income in my 20s because I had a great support network to fall back on. I recognize this is a privilege that many who are interested in being outdoor leaders are not privy to.

NOLS not only set you up well for your favorite style of climbing, but also provided you some other benefits too, right? Yes! I'm so grateful for my time with NOLS. I have some lifelong friends from that. These are fun friends to have. I spent multiple season big wall climbing and highlining in Yosemite and Patagonia with NOLS colleagues. (My proudest climb was doing the Northwest Face of Half Dome in a day, a 22 pitch, Grade VI, 2200 ft big wall.). One time I got an e-mail from a guy I knew from NOLS a bunch of us were copied and he asked if anyone was interested in a canoe trip through Mongolia. I was the only one to respond yes! We spent three months canoeing across Mongolia, Eastern Russia, and Siberia. I flew to Mongolia from Somalia with a winter tent, winter jacket, life jacket, and canoe paddle. (I was working at a maternity ward in Somalia prior to my Siberia expedition). I looked pretty funny walking through this African airport with all that gear. The canoe trip was actually uneventful - everything went right!

You are a ER Physician Assistant - COVID must be pretty challenging for you!
Yes, COVID is definitely in here in Duluth. I'm happy to see people take it seriously and being careful. Especially as younger people, I think we have a responsibility to heed the call to social distance. We need that social connection and we can do it safely.

2020 has been a wild year, a big thing I have chosen to focus on during this year is:

Being a better medical provider. This is a very stressful time for many people, which often manifests in physical symptoms. A big impact of COVID - besides being infected with the disease itself, of course - is the mental health aspect. People are destabilized with all of these huge lifestyle changes that COVID requires. I've seen patients that are manifesting their anxiety as chest pain because these are such trying times. One of our basic needs is human contact. The impact of loneliness is shocking. I am working on being more patient and kind. There's a healthy and appropriate way to connect with others and be outdoors responsibly.

Because of the pandemic, I am spending a lot of my free-time doing:
I'm from Ely and so I'm quite familiar with the Boundary Waters. The lake I live on is half in the designated wilderness area, but the BWCA is big. I am spending my time exploring new lakes in the BWCA. Even though I grew up here, I have grown to appreciate it a lot more and really understand how unique of a wilderness area it is and how lucky we are as northern Minnesotan's to have such a special place as our backyard playground. It is so important that we actively protect it for future generations.

Given your unique perspective from NOLS to ER Medicine, do you have a "call to action" for climbers, specifically? When we were in the Winds, we saw a lot of climbing groups that were leaving trash, camping directly on the trail, and violating Leave No Trace. I would love to see personal accountability and community accountability for our wild areas. If we demand access to these gorgeous outdoor spaces, let's demonstrate that we are worthy of it. Part of Leaving No Trace is having good risk management skills so we can avoid needless rescue situations. This circles back to my hope for our community to continue to be great mentors for each other and willing learners. I am always open to teaching alpine climbing and rescue skills and back country navigation/living skills. I think it's important to "do the outdoors in good style" and be proud of it. This is especially empowering for women.
